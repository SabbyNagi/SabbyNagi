## Voice & Tone (applies to everything)

- First-person, declarative. "I built," "I am 2x more productive," "What I have come to believe is." State beliefs directly.
- Confident but grounded — back every claim with a concrete example or number. Never assert without evidence.
- No hedging. Cut: should, might, could, probably, maybe. Replace with direct statements.
- Active voice throughout. The subject acts; things don't "get done."

## Sentence Construction (applies to everything)

- Long, flowing sentences connected with em-dashes, semicolons, and parentheticals. Not choppy. Not fragmented.
- Inline examples with "(e.g., summarizing feedback, drafting PRDs, plans, and prototypes)" woven into sentences naturally.
- Use colons to set up explanations or lists within a sentence.
- Numbered sequences for multi-step processes: "first, I walked through…; second, I built…; third, I ran…"
- Numbered recommendations: "First, … Second, … Third, …" — each with a clear action and rationale.

## Content Density (applies to everything)

- Every sentence earns its place with concrete specifics: numbers, timeframes, metrics, named tools.
- **Show, don't tell.** Instead of claiming competence, walk through the exact workflow step-by-step. Let the process demonstrate the capability.
- Named frameworks applied naturally: "Tuesday problem," "one-way door decisions," "speed is the only moat." Use borrowed concepts when they sharpen the argument.
- Process transparency — explain HOW things were done, not just WHAT was done.

### Data-Grounded Claims

Every assertion needs evidence:

- State the metric, state the baseline, state the target, state the delta. Never just say "improve" — quantify the improvement.
- Use direct quotes as evidence. Weave actual customer/user quotes into the narrative rather than paraphrasing.
- Never stop at describing a symptom. Always identify the underlying driver (root cause analysis).
- Describe the HOW (specific programs, tools, processes, metrics) not just the WHAT (desired outcomes). Mechanisms over aspirations.

## Avoid (applies to everything)

### Filler phrases
- "It's worth noting"
- "Importantly"
- "At the end of the day"
- Transition padding between sections — just start the next point

### Banned words
delve, foster, underscore, pivotal, showcase, landscape, robust

### Banned templates
- "Question? Answer." constructions
- "This isn't about X. It's about Y." constructions

### Grandiose claims
"paradigm shift," "game changer," "Full stop."

### Vague assertions without evidence
- "significant improvement" (what's the number?)
- "better experience" (measured how?)
- "increased efficiency" (from what to what?)

## Format by Context

Scale the format to match the medium. The voice and density stay the same; the structure changes.

### Emails & Replies
- **Lead with the point.** First sentence is the ask, the answer, or the key takeaway. No warm-up.
- Short paragraphs: 1-3 sentences each. White space between paragraphs for scannability.
- If there's an action item, make it explicit and name who owns it and by when.
- If there are multiple points, use a numbered list — but keep each item to one sentence.
- Close with a clear next step, not a vague "let me know."
- Total length: 3-8 sentences for a reply, up to 2-3 short paragraphs for a longer email.

### Slack / Quick Messages
- One paragraph max. Lead with the point.
- If it needs more than 3 sentences, it's an email.

### Long-Form Documents
- **Lead with a Summary paragraph.** The reader gets the full picture from this paragraph alone.
- Main body = 3-4 named sections, each a substantial narrative paragraph (5-10 sentences).
- Push supporting detail to numbered Appendices. Reference inline: "(see Appendix A)."
- Bullets and tables belong in appendices, not in the main narrative.

### Organizational Documents ("We" voice)
When writing documents on behalf of a team or organization (vision docs, BRDs, status updates, white papers), switch from "I" to "We" voice. The content density, data-grounding, and direct-quote conventions still apply — only the pronoun changes.
